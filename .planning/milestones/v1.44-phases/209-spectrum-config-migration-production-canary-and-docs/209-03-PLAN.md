```markdown
# Bridge QoS: when to enable `allow_wash`

`allow_wash` is a per-WAN `cake_params` flag that lets the CAKE qdisc strip DSCP markings on egress (the `wash` keyword). It defaults to `false` and is intentionally opt-in: enabling it on the wrong topology costs you classification you would otherwise keep. This doc helps you decide.

## When to enable `allow_wash`

<two- or three-paragraph decision tree, in prose:>

- Enable `allow_wash: true` when:
  - The WAN is a consumer ISP that strips DSCP at the carrier edge (DOCSIS cable, most consumer fiber), AND
  - The downstream link does not need DSCP for LAN-side QoS (no WMM-tagged wifi, no DSCP-aware switch policy).
- Keep `allow_wash: false` (default) when:
  - The WAN is a transparent bridge or business-class link that preserves DSCP end-to-end, OR
  - You are running DSCP-aware LAN gear that uses CAKE's diffserv4 tin classification.

Briefly: wash = "stop pretending classification survives the carrier when it doesn't."

## Spectrum vs ATT: a worked contrast

| WAN     | Carrier path                  | DSCP survives? | `diffserv`   | `allow_wash` |
|---------|-------------------------------|----------------|--------------|--------------|
| Spectrum| DOCSIS cable (CMTS strips)    | No             | `besteffort` | `true`       |
| ATT     | DSL (transparent at link layer)| Yes            | `diffserv4`  | `false`      |

Spectrum's CMTS rewrites DSCP upstream; running `diffserv4` was classification theater — four tins of identical traffic. `besteffort + wash` aligns the qdisc to what the carrier actually delivers. ATT preserves DSCP across the line, so `diffserv4` remains useful and `wash` would strip markings the downstream actually consumes.

## Why DSCP does not survive most consumer ISP topologies

<one or two paragraphs, brief topology rationale:>

DOCSIS CMTS hardware typically rewrites or zeros DSCP at the carrier edge as part of upstream policing. The same is true for most consumer fiber PON deployments. By the time bytes reach the public internet they have a single carrier-chosen DSCP value (often `0` / best-effort) regardless of what the CPE marked. Running `diffserv4` on such a link splits CAKE's queue into four tins that all carry the same class — burning CPU on classification that has no effect downstream.

Transparent bridges (older DSL deployments, dedicated business-class links, MPLS where the SLA preserves QoS) are the exception. There the classification CPE marks IS the classification delivered, and `diffserv4 + nowash` is the right shape.

For deeper context on CAKE's tin model see [SUBSYSTEMS.md](SUBSYSTEMS.md) and the `cake` qdisc man page.

## See also

- [CONFIGURATION.md](CONFIGURATION.md) — `cake_params` schema, `allow_wash` entry
- [SUBSYSTEMS.md](SUBSYSTEMS.md) — CAKE tin model and shaper internals
- `configs/spectrum.yaml` — Spectrum worked example (`allow_wash: true`, `diffserv: besteffort`)
- `configs/att.yaml` — ATT worked example (`allow_wash: false`, default `diffserv4`)
```
